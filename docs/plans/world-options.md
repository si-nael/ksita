# World Options

## Purpose
The world is not locked yet. Choose the world's law before choosing the story.

These options are intentionally different at the structural level, not just in surface art direction. Pick based on which law, pressure, and relationship distortion feels strongest.

## How To Choose
- Choose by what the world demands from people, not by plot convenience.
- Choose by which visual and ritual language feels unforgettable.
- Choose by which option creates the most twisted and necessary relationships.
- Reject any option whose shortest description feels replaceable.

## Selected Direction | The Hollow

### Locked Core Law
The world is a void, and its physics do not depend on time. Nothing changes because time passes. Distance, heat, growth, decay, motion, and death change only when relations of witness, weight, naming, and position are rewritten.

### Locked Representative Place
The Sea Beneath the Last Stair.

### Why It Won
- it is immediately non-terrestrial
- it forces every system to feel unlike Earth by default
- it creates a hard mechanical identity instead of just a lore identity
- it can make relationships physically dangerous without using familiar fantasy institutions as the main engine

### Note
The older options remain below as archive material, not the active direction.

## Option A | The Choir Realm Of Tithed True Names

### Core Law
The realm survives by binding surrendered true names into choir-spires grown from dead god-glass. Tithed names stabilize roads, bloodlines, weather, harvest, and sacred borders.

### What Makes It Beautiful
- pilgrim roads that sing underfoot
- candlelit abbeys and glass-root towers
- a world that feels ancient, ceremonial, and tragically maintained

### What Makes It Cruel
- people survive by letting institutions prune them into singular, acceptable selves
- erased names can still haunt inheritance, prayer, and territory
- love becomes dangerous when it preserves someone the world needs simplified

### Relationship Fuel
- replacement heirs
- guardians preparing loved ones as ritual vessels
- lovers who keep one another illegally real
- siblings who cannot both be recognized by the same house or shrine

### Iconic Image
A choir-spire ringing at dusk while a manor opens its doors for an erased heir no law should remember.

### Distinctive Strength
Strong tragic grandeur and high symbolic power without relying on generic monarchy fantasy.

### Main Risk
If handled lazily, it can slide toward familiar church-corruption or bloodline fantasy.

## Option B | The Garden Kingdoms Of Shed Selves

### Core Law
People do not keep one body for life. Sacred gardens grow successive bodies from seed-flesh, memory sap, and ancestral grafts. At key life stages, a person molts into a new body and the old one is buried, stored, or repurposed.

### What Makes It Beautiful
- moonlit skin orchards and bone-white conservatories
- ceremonial molting festivals
- a realm where age, beauty, grief, and status are all horticultural arts

### What Makes It Cruel
- society insists one body is the legal person while older selves are treated as residue, relic, or raw material
- nobles can splice ideal heirs from discarded lineage-flesh
- people may preserve the parts of someone they love while destroying the person's continuity

### Relationship Fuel
- a parent raising a child inside a dead sibling's grown body
- lovers keeping each other's previous selves in hidden gardens
- caretakers who prune undesirable traits before a molt
- rivals fighting over which body is the "true" continuation of the same person

### Iconic Image
A moon garden full of dormant former selves hanging like fruit, each still able to whisper when touched.

### Distinctive Strength
Extremely original body-and-identity pressure with strong visual identity and built-in twisted intimacy.

### Main Risk
It can become too abstract or too body-horror heavy unless the emotional logic stays precise.

## Option C | The Leviathan Pilgrimage Sea

### Core Law
The world is an archipelago riding on chained, sleeping leviathans. Islands exist only because massive dream-beasts remain asleep. To keep them dormant, kingdoms tithe unlived futures, unborn lineages, and unfulfilled vows into anchor shrines.

### What Makes It Beautiful
- cathedrals lashed to moving backs of sea giants
- tide-lit ports, whale-bone bridges, dream lantern flotillas
- a world where the horizon always feels alive and temporary

### What Makes It Cruel
- every stable homeland is bought with futures that will never happen
- waking one leviathan can free truth and doom thousands
- entire families may exist only because another bloodline was never allowed to be born

### Relationship Fuel
- someone promised as a human anchor to the beast beneath their homeland
- siblings where one exists only because the other's future was tithed away
- captains or priests who love a person and the leviathan that will eventually consume them
- lovers split between two islands that cannot safely draw near each other

### Iconic Image
An entire pilgrimage city swaying in moonlit silence as the eye of the beast beneath it slowly opens.

### Distinctive Strength
Huge scale, immediate awe, and a world law that naturally creates sacrifice without feeling like standard kingdom politics.

### Main Risk
The scale can become expensive or diffuse if early chapters are not tightly localized.

## Option D | The Devouring Houses

### Core Law
The great Houses are not dynasties alone. They are sentient manors grown from ancestor remains, oath timber, and buried beasts. Each House protects its territory from outer hunger, but only if fed marriages, heirs, secrets, and bloodline pruning.

### What Makes It Beautiful
- living palaces with breathing halls and ember-veined walls
- heraldic beasts nesting in stairwells
- a courtly world where architecture itself remembers and judges

### What Makes It Cruel
- a House can love its residents and still digest their identities for continuity
- adoption, marriage, and inheritance are forms of controlled consumption
- no heir belongs wholly to themselves if the House has chosen them

### Relationship Fuel
- a person beloved by the House more than by their own family
- marriage as ritual ingestion into another bloodline organism
- siblings who are not rivals by choice but by the appetite of the same manor
- a caretaker who serves both as parent and as mouthpiece for the House's hunger

### Iconic Image
A bride entering a manor's heartroom while the walls pulse around her like something trying to remember her taste.

### Distinctive Strength
Very strong setting identity and direct fusion of family, property, inheritance, and predation.

### Main Risk
If every conflict stays inside noble spaces, the world may feel too narrow.

## Option E | The Bellwood Of Stolen Roles

### Core Law
The ancient forest does not understand individuals. It understands roles. Mother, hunter, bride, saint, king, executioner, guide. Villages survive by ringing seasonal role-bells that assign people into protected archetypes. Anyone left unassigned becomes vulnerable to the wild role-spirits outside the wards.

### What Makes It Beautiful
- bell towers above black pines and red moss ravines
- ritual pageants where the village becomes a moving myth
- a world that feels folkloric, intimate, and predatory

### What Makes It Cruel
- safety depends on performing a role that may have nothing to do with who you are
- love becomes dangerous if it violates the forest's assigned pattern
- the world can preserve a relationship only by renaming it into something unbearable

### Relationship Fuel
- a sibling forced into the role of parent over the other
- a beloved assigned as your executioner each winter
- someone who keeps ringing the same bell to preserve a bond the world would otherwise unmake
- two people who can only remain near each other if one stops being fully themselves

### Iconic Image
A winter bell procession where every villager wears a sacred role-mask except the one person the forest refuses to name.

### Distinctive Strength
Strong folklore identity, very twisted relationship pressure, and a clean world law with immediate symbolism.

### Main Risk
It can drift into pure allegory unless grounded by concrete daily life and material stakes.

## Recommendation Filter
If you want the most aristocratic and tragic option, choose Option A.

If you want the strangest identity mechanics and the most inherently twisted intimacy, choose Option B.

If you want the biggest awe and most mythic scale, choose Option C.

If you want the strongest family-property-predation fusion, choose Option D.

If you want the sharpest folklore pressure on relationships and roles, choose Option E.

## Next Step After Selection
Once one option is chosen, do only these three things next:
