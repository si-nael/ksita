# First Arc Direction

## Purpose
Lock the first perspective anchor, the first arc tone, and the opening-sequence direction so the project starts feeling like a real work instead of a loose setting bible.

## Chosen Perspective Anchor
Lune.

## Why Lune Wins
- Lune moves through the world naturally, so exposition can happen through action and route labor instead of lectures
- Lune stands between sanctioned witness and private Keeping without starting as a neutral blank slate
- Lune can plausibly encounter Iri, Nao, Yun, and Sera in one arc without the setup feeling forced
- Lune is visually cool enough to anchor the project's first impression
- Lune's private damage already contains the right hook: they are tired of pretending delivery is neutral

## Lune POV Rules
- Lune should notice route truth, body states, and social danger before abstract philosophy
- Lune should read people in terms of what they are holding together or letting break
- Lune should not sound like a lore narrator; their voice should feel tired, sharp, and emotionally evasive

## First Arc Tone
Seductive-tragic first, predatory second.

### What That Means
- the first arc should feel beautiful and emotionally magnetic before it feels monstrous
- Keeping should first read as intimate and tempting, then reveal its possessive and destructive cost
- the Common Vigil should first read as elegant and stabilizing, then reveal how cold that stability can become
- outright horror should arrive after attachment is already in place

## Opening Sequence Direction

### Sequence Goal
Introduce Hollow traversal, Witness readability, Lune's role, and the Returning conflict in one uninterrupted pull.

### Beat 1: Carrying In Motion
Open on Lune running witness across the underside piers of the Sea Beneath the Last Stair.

What this proves:
- the world is impossible
- route labor is stylish
- Lune is competent before being explained

### Beat 2: Sanctioned Beauty
The Vigil Span looks almost holy for a moment: aligned bells, clean witness light, stable crossing geometry, Iri's public presence in the distance.

What this proves:
- the sanctioned world is genuinely attractive
- players understand what "correct" witness feels like

### Beat 3: Wrong Light
Nao's backcall begins from a blind-side pier or hidden hold point. Lune notices the route respond before the crowd does.

What this proves:
- Keeping is not abstract; it has immediate sensory force
- Lune is good enough to read danger early

### Beat 4: Yun Splits The Bridge
Yun becomes visibly Twice-Called. The body and shadow stop agreeing about where home is.

What this proves:
- the emotional conflict is also physical law
- Yun is not passive scenery but a disaster point with desire inside it

### Beat 5: Lune Holds The Middle
The bridge remains crossable only because Lune keeps carrying the route through a state that should already have failed.

What this proves:
- Lune matters
- neutrality in this world is violent labor

### Beat 6: Sera Arrives Too Late
Sera reaches the conflict when the right decision no longer exists cleanly.

What this proves:
- law enters after feeling has already rewritten the scene
- Sera is dangerous even when trying to prevent worse damage

### Beat 7: Cut On Wrong Home
End the sequence on the first undeniable image that something irreversible happened: Yun's shadow, step, or answer-name chooses Nao before the body does.

What this proves:
- Returning is the right first conflict
- the player now wants to know what kind of world allows this

## First Arc Promise
The player should leave the opening with these reactions:
- Lune is cool and exhausted in a way worth following
- Nao is impossible to dismiss as just wrong
- Iri is beautiful but already cracked
- Yun is dangerous to care about
- Sera makes every scene colder the moment they enter

## Design Rule
If the first arc feels explanatory before it feels magnetic, rebuild it.
