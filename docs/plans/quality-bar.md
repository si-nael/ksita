# Quality Bar

## Purpose
"Legendary" is not a useful production instruction by itself. This document converts that ambition into enforceable standards.

## Non-Negotiable Standards
- No chapter should survive if its core plot can be predicted from the first clean summary.
- No major reveal should exist unless it sharpens at least three earlier scenes.
- No lore scene should remain if it does not immediately change a relationship, location, or decision.
- No major antagonist force should be evil for its own sake. It must preserve something genuinely necessary.
- No central relationship should exist only to provide comfort, exposition, or motivation.
- No recurring image, ritual, or space should feel exchangeable with another fantasy game.
- No major role or faction should survive if it feels conceptually neat but not character-magnetic.
- No document or scene should explain the world in a way that makes it sound less attractive than it should feel in motion.
- No visual direction should survive if it makes the project look more generic as production value rises.
- No playtime target should be met through filler; extra hours must come from reinterpretation, mastery, or route divergence.
- No chapter should survive if the player's main contribution is only reading, choosing dialogue, and walking to the next scene.
- No mechanic should survive if it can be replaced by a menu choice or a single switch interaction without losing the project's identity.
- No failure state should default to death-and-reload if misarrival, wrong return, drift, or altered standing would express the Hollow better.
- No mechanic should survive just because it is original on paper; it must be fun quickly in the player's hands.
- No major climax should surrender player control exactly when the music and emotion are supposed to peak.
- No major reveal should depend mainly on hidden trivia if a cleaner lawful reframe would hit harder.

## Scene-Level Bar
Each important scene should achieve at least two of these:

- introduce a world law through action
- alter the reader's understanding of a relationship
- create a memorable visual or ritual image
- force a cost-bearing choice
- reframe a previously safe place
- force the player to perform a world-law-specific action under pressure
- fuse gameplay, music, and consequence into a single emotional beat

If a scene only communicates plot, it is underbuilt.

## Chapter-Level Bar
Each chapter should leave behind all of these:

- one image or ritual that only this game could own
- one relationship wound that cannot be neatly healed
- one world change the player can revisit and feel
- one truth that makes the next chapter morally harder instead of easier
- one character, role, or pairing that feels worth digging into beyond the chapter itself
- one moment where beauty and dread intensify each other rather than cancel each other out

## Plot Bar
- Avoid prophecy, sealed-evil, throne-claim, and corruption-reveal skeletons unless the structure has been broken hard enough to become unrecognizable.
- Avoid twists that work only because information was hidden, rather than because meaning changed.
- Avoid final answers that flatten contradiction into a clean moral lesson.

## Prototype Questions
- Can a player explain why the world is beautiful and unforgivable at the same time?
- Does at least one bond feel more upsetting after a tender scene rather than before it?
- Does the world seem impossible to summarize as "like X and Y" without losing what makes it itself?
- Is there at least one image, rite, or rule that someone would remember months later?
- Is there at least one role, design, or pairing someone would latch onto as a favorite?
- Would a still frame of the project look unmistakable even without animation or effects?

## Kill Rule
If an idea is merely good, familiar, or efficient but not singular, cut it or mutate it until it earns its place.
