# Creative Boundaries

## Purpose
Use this document to prevent drift into obvious imitation. Inspiration is allowed. Structural borrowing is not.

## Non-Negotiable Boundaries
- Do not build the game around a hidden pacifist-versus-massacre route binary.
- Do not rely on a single mercy menu choice as the primary emotional mechanic.
- Do not use mascot-contract structures, magical-girl hierarchy, or surreal witch-hunt framing.
- Do not make the core loop a school-club routine by day and symbolic dungeon by night unless a future prototype proves a genuinely new structural reason for it.
- Do not lean on meta-humor, player guilt tricks, or retro-RPG parody as the main source of distinctiveness.
- Do not create confrontation scenes that are only thinly disguised bullet-hell boss fights with new narrative paint.
- Do not reduce the setting to a modern bureaucracy with fantasy skin.
- Do not fall back on chosen-one prophecy, sealed ancient evil, generic church corruption, or throne-war skeletons.
- Do not use a clean betrayal twist as the main source of surprise.
- Do not write core bonds as safe friend-rival-mentor-romance archetypes with only darker dialogue on top.
- Do not let major places read like Earth locations with extra ornaments.
- Do not treat the achronal law as simple time travel, rewind, or timeline branching.
- Do not explain the void through pseudo-scientific cosmology that drains its fantasy weight.
- Do not let the tone drift into dusty pseudo-classical prose or medieval-literature cosplay.
- Do not let the project read like a worldbuilding essay that forgot to become a character-driven work.
- Do not make the setting intellectually interesting but emotionally unfavoriteable.
- Do not default to photoreal rendering or generic anime-style 3D just because it seems more market-safe.
- Do not pursue volumetric depth if it weakens silhouette identity.

## Positive Identity Rules
- Originality should come from world law, not surface aesthetics alone.
- The setting itself must create pressure on relationships.
- Confrontations should feel like negotiating with an intelligent sacred system, not just defeating monsters.
- Emotional force should come from re-authorized reality: what the world allows to exist, not just what characters confess.
- Reference works may be used as emotional benchmarks, but only at the level of feeling, cadence, and structural pressure, never at the level of copied plot or cast architecture.
- The world must remain beautiful enough that preserving it is a tempting choice.
- Fantasy should feel ancient, ceremonial, and materially specific rather than generic.
- Core bonds should combine tenderness with desecration, necessity, and asymmetry.
- The world must feel physically impossible before any plot explanation begins.
- The physics rule must change ordinary actions, not just lore text.
- Roles, props, and titles should be strong enough to make people pick favorites.
- The world should invite character obsession and theorycrafting, not only admiration.
- Art direction should privilege iconic still-frame readability over realism.

## Smell Test
Reject or rework an idea if the cleanest description sounds like one of these:

- "It is basically a kindness route against a boss."
- "It is basically a magical-girl-style descent into symbolic nightmare space."
- "It is basically a quirky RPG that hides darkness under jokes."
- "It is basically a chosen one against an evil church."
- "It is basically a dark fantasy kingdom with memory magic layered on top."
- "It is basically a normal fantasy world except time is weird."
- "It sounds like a cool wiki page more than a world with irresistible characters."

## Replacement Strategy
If an idea fails the smell test, replace at least one of these layers before moving forward:

1. World law
2. Player verbs
3. Conflict framing
4. Spatial logic
5. Emotional cost

If a reference point is being used, also replace at least one of these:

6. music-function relationship
7. reveal logic
